12 Things to Know Before Your First Trip to Tokyo

Travel tips · ~6 min read

Tokyo is easier than it looks and stranger than you expect. It runs on quiet rules more than written ones. Get a handful of them right and the city opens up fast. Here are the twelve I'd tell a friend before their first trip.

1. Carry cash — more than you'd think

Tokyo feels futuristic, but a lot of small restaurants, temples and shops are still cash-only. Pull yen from a 7-Eleven ATM (they take foreign cards when bank machines won't) and always keep a few thousand yen on you.

2. Get a Suica or Pasmo on day one

This rechargeable card taps you through every train and most convenience stores. It saves you from decoding fare charts at every station, which you will otherwise do, badly.

3. The trains stop around midnight

Miss the last train and you're choosing between a taxi that costs a fortune or waiting out the night. Check the last departure before a long dinner — it's earlier than a city this size should allow.

4. Stand on the correct side of the escalator

In Tokyo you stand left, walk right. Get it backwards and you'll feel the polite frustration of a hundred commuters behind you.

5. Silence on the train is the norm

Phone calls are a faux pas, and loud conversation marks you instantly as a visitor. Put your phone on silent — "manner mode" — like everyone else.

6. Convenience stores are genuinely good

Don't laugh — 7-Eleven, Lawson and FamilyMart do fresh, cheap, surprisingly excellent food. A konbini breakfast is a legitimate plan, not a compromise.

7. Tipping isn't a thing

Don't tip. It can cause genuine confusion, and in some places it's mildly awkward for the staff. Good service is just the baseline here.

8. Learn three phrases

"Sumimasen" (excuse me / sorry), "arigatou gozaimasu" (thank you), and "eigo?" (English?). That's enough to be polite and get help almost anywhere.

9. Google Maps knows the trains cold

It gives you platform numbers, car positions and transfer times. Trust it — Tokyo's rail map looks like spilled spaghetti until an app untangles it for you.

10. Pick neighbourhoods, not a checklist

Tokyo is a stack of villages. Spend a morning fully in Shimokitazawa or Yanaka instead of sprinting between famous crossings. The city rewards depth over breadth.

11. Slurping is fine

At a ramen counter, slurping is normal and even a small compliment. Eat fast, eat loud, free up the seat — that's the etiquette.

12. Everything is cleaner than seems possible — and there are no bins

Public rubbish bins are rare, yet the streets are spotless. The trick: people carry their trash home. Keep a small bag for wrappers and do the same.
