What Is a VPN, and Do You Actually Need One?

Technology explainer · ~5 min read

Quick answer: a VPN hides your browsing from your internet provider and the Wi-Fi you're on, and makes it look like you're somewhere else. It does not make you anonymous or hacker-proof. Useful on public Wi-Fi and for streaming region locks — oversold for almost everything else.

VPN ads run on fear: shadowy hackers, stolen identities, a countdown timer on your privacy. Most of it is marketing. A VPN is a genuinely useful tool for a few specific jobs — and useless for the ones the ads imply. Here's the honest version.

What a VPN actually does

Think of it as a private tunnel for your internet traffic. Normally, your internet provider (and the coffee-shop Wi-Fi) can see which sites you visit. A VPN encrypts that traffic and routes it through a server somewhere else, so they see gibberish going to one address instead. To the websites you visit, you appear to be wherever that server is.

Where it's genuinely worth it

Public Wi-Fi. Airports, cafés, hotels — networks you don't control. A VPN stops others on that network from snooping on what you're doing. This is the strongest real reason to use one.

Region-locked content. Want a show that's only on a streaming library in another country? A VPN can make you appear to be there.

Hiding activity from your provider. If you'd rather your ISP not log every site you visit, a VPN moves that visibility to the VPN company instead — which is why which company matters.

What it does NOT do

It doesn't make you anonymous — you're still logged into your accounts, and trackers still follow those. It doesn't stop viruses or phishing. And it doesn't hide anything from a VPN provider that keeps logs. "No-logs" claims are only as good as the company's honesty and audits.

So, do you need one?

If you regularly use public Wi-Fi, or you want to sidestep region locks — yes, a reputable paid VPN is worth the few dollars a month. If you just browse at home on your own connection and expected a VPN to make you invisible, the honest answer is: not really. Skip the fear, buy it for the job it actually does.
