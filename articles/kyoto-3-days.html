3 Perfect Days in Kyoto: A First-Timer's Itinerary

Travel itinerary · ~7 min read

The plan: east Kyoto and its temples on day one, the bamboo west and a food street on day two, and a slower day three for the shrine everyone photographs plus whatever you fell in love with. Built to go early, beat the crowds, and stop zig-zagging across the city.

Kyoto has more than a thousand temples, and first-timers usually try to see too many of them. You don't need to. Three focused days, arranged so you're not crossing the city twice a day, will show you the best of it without turning your holiday into a checklist. Here's how I'd spend them.

Day 1 — Eastern Kyoto (Higashiyama)

Start early at Kiyomizu-dera, ideally by 8am. This is the temple that ends up on every postcard, and by mid-morning the approach is shoulder-to-shoulder. Walk down through Sannenzaka and Ninenzaka — two preserved lanes of wooden shopfronts — while they're still quiet.

From there, drift north through Higashiyama toward Gion, the old geisha district. Afternoon is for wandering: tea houses, the Shirakawa canal, and if you time it right, a glimpse of a geiko heading to an appointment around dusk. Don't chase them for photos — locals are tired of it, and it's a fast way to look like a tourist.

Day 2 — Western Kyoto (Arashiyama) and Nishiki

Head west early to the Arashiyama Bamboo Grove. Same rule as yesterday: get there before 9am or share the path with a hundred other cameras. Nearby, Tenryu-ji has one of the finest gardens in the city and a fraction of the foot traffic.

Back in the centre for the afternoon, walk Nishiki Market — five covered blocks of pickles, tofu, grilled skewers and things you won't recognise. Graze your way through instead of sitting down for one big meal; that's the point of it.

Day 3 — Fushimi Inari and a Slower Finish

Give the morning to Fushimi Inari, the shrine with thousands of vermilion gates climbing the hillside. Go at sunrise if you can face it — the lower gates are mobbed by 10am, but almost nobody walks the full loop up the mountain. Twenty minutes of climbing buys you the photo everyone wants, alone.

Keep the afternoon loose. Go back to the neighbourhood that grabbed you, find a small kissaten (old-school coffee house), and let Kyoto be a city you sat in rather than sprinted through.

A Few Practical Notes

Buy an IC card (ICOCA) for buses and trains — Kyoto's buses are the workhorse and fumbling for change slows everyone down. Temples open early and close by 5pm, so mornings are your currency here. And wear shoes you can slip off; you'll be doing it a lot.
